Vessel Production

Product - Vessel Science

Origins

After the launch of Vessel Research, we approached industry-side (Biotech and Pharma) researchers and found that they were also plagued by similar issues that academic researchers faced.

Documentation systems were poor if not unusable. Collaboration tools were outdated. In fact, pen and paper was still used to record data even in pharma giants such as Allergan.

The batch record was at the heart of the problem. Every drug or medical device produced requires a batch record. This record tracks all procedures, equipment and ingredients used for production.

This documentation is mandated by federal law. Even drug production at the research and trial phase required this.
Larger companies used Oracle and SAP systems to track inventory. Sometimes, custom software was built on top of these enterprise solutions to manage batch records, but this was rare. Most companies still relied on paper worksheets that were printed and filled in by hand.

The Problem

A bevy of problems existed with the current paper batch record:
  1. Illegible handwriting led to lost or misunderstood data
  2. Human error caused incorrect manual chemical calculations and conversions
  3. Difficult to track versions and changes of a batch worksheet
  4. Difficult to track and organize all batch worksheets for product lines
  5. Loss of data integrity: records could be falsely modified afterwards
  6. A new batch worksheet must be made for every product: this led to unneeded manual duplication
The friction generated by a pen & paper system resulted in real bottom-line implications. Improper, wrong, or missing documentation directly led to FDA fines:
In fact, pharma and medical device companies have been fined over $16 billion in the last decade.
A cloud & mobile based batch documentation system could empower researchers and production line workers to efficiently document their work.


We wanted biotechs to move fast while maintaining regulatory compliance and reducing errors.

Participatory Design

Our first client was TekNova, a small biotech that produced basic gels and media plates used for research and drug production.
TekNova used a paper batch system. Their customizable product line exacerbated "batch record creep": each customized product required a new worksheet and it soon became impossible to track all these variations and changes.

I first conducted open-ended whiteboarding sessions with TekNova's CEO. I asked him to explain the product line. I focused on getting him to draw out his ideal mental model of a batch and not the current paper representation.
After a few whiteboarding sessions, I noticed some underlying patterns:
  1. A universal set of actions was used across all products (weighing, cleaning, sterilizing...)
  2. The same action across products varied by a set of parameters. For example, the sterilization step might vary on required temperature different products.
  3. Base components (ingredients) used for each product was always recorded in the same manner
I used these observations as the base of the action framework: a scalable, descriptive, and flexible building block to design and describe batch records.

Information Architecture

A product template would be the digital representation of a batch worksheet.

Users could fork and branch existing templates to easily reflect customized and branched product lines.
A product template consists of:
  1. List of components
  2. Sequence of actions performed on components
The list of components would contain all the necessary compliance metadata and chemical calculations for each product.
The action is the building block for defining the procedures for a batch.
Parameters are the discrete values that define a procedure. For example, sterilization would have temperature, duration, and location as parameters. The sterilization step for different products will have different values for each parameter.

Notes are for free-form text to better describe a procedure.

Signoffs are checkpoints that require a digital signature for compliance purposes.
Each organization would have a library of common actions.
Users would be able to stitch together actions to create a template.
When put together, actions and components clearly articulate a production workflow.

Participatory Design (Part II)

I conducted a participatory card-sorting session with TekNova's Batch and QA managers to test this framework.

I printed out actual action cards with common TekNova procedures. Then, I asked the participants to use the cards to articulate actual products.
This exercise tested the expressibility and scalability of the action framework. Could our subjects design batch records for the entire range of TekNova products?
For the most part, our subjects found the framework to be powerful and expressive.
Minor changes were made based on feedback:
  1. We incorporated components into actions; our subjects found that this provided better context.
  2. We removed the concept of separate signoffs and automatically added signoffs for each parameter or component.

The Production Workflow

We had just designed the format of batch records/worksheets. Next, we had to contextualize this artifact within the greater production workflow.

After high level interviews with biotech production managers I extrapolated a high-level process.
During development, batch and QA managers are responsible for creating the necessary actions and templates to mirror each product line.

In execution, technicians "ran the template". These technicians were responsible for recording data for each parameter and making the necessary digital signature signoffs. At this point, we called the "executable template" a batch in our software.

During analysis, production managers are responsible for monitoring current and completed batch runs. Production managers had to approve/reject completed batches and respond to issues or escalations. We built dashboards for this part of the workflow.

Development

Vessel Production was built on top of the existing Vessel Research stack.

This was an enterprise grade application holding mission critical data so a higher emphasis was put on testing.
I used Mocha to run server-side unit tests and end-to-end API integration tests. Karma was used to run UI client-side UI tests. Codeship tied everything together for continous-integration.
The mobile application played an important role. Recording batch data would be primarily accomplished through the phone due to the physical nature of drug production. I decided to solely focus on developing an iOS application and used React Native to leverage native performance.

The Regulatory Environment

FDA compliance is the central motivation for maintaining batch records.

We were fortunate to have FDA regulatory expert, Angela Bazigos, to consult and guide us through this process. Angela has decades of compliance experience and was part of the original team that wrote CFR 11 Part 21 - the official guideline for digital FDA compliance.

Much of the onus of compliance comes from the actual documentation of batch records, so I built in safeguards to guide users towards compliance.
Technicians would be warned if expired components are being used.
Issues would be automatically generated for compliance red flags such as high variance from parameter values.
Visual revision history help batch managers to track changes.

Design Principles

Our design principles were heavily guided by the legal and regulatory environment.

One goal of Vessel was to minimize failed batches. This meant giving guidance and warning whenever bad values were being filled out.

On the other hand, we did not want to restrict input. Ultimately, technicians should truthfully record whatever they did. A failure to do so would be a breach of federal law.

This was my conclusion:
  1. Always give guidance in UI wherever possible to avoid non-compliance.
  2. Let users enter whatever they wish, even if it is wrong.
  3. Track issues and create an audit trail for future reference.
I wanted Vessel to personify a wise basketball coach. One who gives advice without interrupting the game. One who lets the players play.
Kudos if you get the Phil "Zen Master" Jackson reference.

Outcomes

Vessel Production is currently in private beta among small scale bio-manufacturers.

The core application is complete and the current challenge is for full CFR 21 part 11 compliance and validation. Much of this has to do with the underlying cloud and hardware infrastructure of our application.

As a designer, I realized that these development challenges were out of my scope. Despite the fact I had designed and implemented the core features, we needed a more seasoned developer who had experience in setting up infrastructure in regulated environments.

I also realized that I wanted to focus more on design than development, so I worked with my CEO for a transition out.
